Internship summary

.pptx version

  • Approach
  • Problems I encountered
  • Result
I began reading whatever I could get my hands on in terms of research. I quickly realized that establishing a specific benchmark for such a project is very challenging, what's good and what's bad? As one might expect, there is also little or no information accessible about such projects because they are rarely made public. I had to start from scratch and so I did.
I had to chose technical data (historical price data) for the reason being that there are many restrictions on fundamental data (company data). I quickly found out that prediction quality is rather bad when you work mere with price, because of the few patterns incorporated solely in ohlc-data.

The challenges I was facing had mostly to do with the kind of market I was trying to forecast, along with the way I defined my algorithm's output. I immediately started with the cryptocurrency market as a target. The reason being that if my model performs under huge volatility (crypto), it should also succeed on less volatile instruments (stocks).

Through a series of heterogeneous measurements, I transformed the original ohlc data into a more feature-rich set of data. Along with the above, converting to a categorical output rather than predicting price itself also improved my algorithm by a tremendous amount.
You can only judge the quality of a strategy when you have backtested it extensively. It is for this reason that I compared the performance of my algorithm against a strong baseline.

As a baseline, I chose a random signal generator, which returns either ‘hold’ or ‘buy’ regardless of market conditions. At first glance, this sounds like a bad baseline, but it actually isn't. If by chance one had bought a few times during the last few months, due to the exceptional market conditions one had made a profit without having any idea of what they were actually doing.

Despite the market conditions, our model even managed to beat the baseline by a fair amount.
